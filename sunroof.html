MG-Rover Technical and Information Pages 

General maintenance, cooling and fuel system, cabin and engine fuse box listing, audio remotes, ignition, navigation, vehicle monitoring and colour codes, along with various other information of the Rover 200 and MGZR

Noticed recently the front corner of the MG passenger side roof headlining was showing signs of being damp after a heavy and sustained rainfall, water would collect in that area, Investigating this problem before the winter weather sets in, showed that a build of hard sediment, over the years, had formed within the front nearside drainage tube, a damp headlining is bad news at anytime, more so in the winter months, 

A length of plastic coated curtain wire broke down the sediment while pushing through as shown, the wire wont go through the whole length due to a tight bend in the lower section. By just pulling back the wheel arch liner with steering on full left lock gave easy access to complete the lower part cleaning out process.

A blocked drain tube, may be a common damp problem on older vehicles, especially with damaged or worn weather strips, Drainage tubes situated at each corner should allow water to run off the vehicle which is then expelled onto the road from under each of the wheel arches.

If damp does persists in any other areas, it could be a split tube within the head lining which supplies water for rear screen wash. Even the base seal of the vehicles roof mounted aerial. However one of the most ignored areas are the bodywork to roof, factory welded seams.

In some rare cases these may reveal suspect areas in each of these two channels, to remove the plastic trims, thumb pressure is applied from windscreen end, then push back and detach each of the two roof trims.

If the sunroof seal does need to be replaced. Which had to be done on my own car a couple of years ago, the glass panel was put into the tilted position, then pushed off the 2 plastic side trims that cover the mechanism to expose the securing bolts, 3 to each side. Using a T.25 star bit remove all the bolts, pushed panel up from inside car as helper retrieves it, remove old seal and cleaned fitment area.

With glass panel face down on a blanket covered flat working area, using a small rotary wire brush attached to the dremel, all rust areas especially the groove which the seal fits into are cleaned up, surface rust on back edge of the glass frame treated to hammerite smooth black, then applied silicone sealant to the new seal. After bonding seal to the glass, making sure the rubber joint was at the 'rear' this was then left to cure for a time, while the exposed folding sunroof mechanism was treated to some copper ease, before fitting the glass panel back.


Before panel is placed back into roof aperture, tilt mechanism now needed to be in the 'closed' position, with the glass panel centralised, and level with the car roof, each of the six bolts can be inserted and tightened, testing the roof operation is needed before the plastic side trims are fitted back, if any further adjustments were needed. It is possible that the bolts can be inserted and tightened, with roof in closed position. Good preperation and using a quality silicone sealant should make this fitting a one off for the life of the vehicle, A new type of seal thats available now has a 'soft close' velvet edge, compared to the older original ones.  

A well used worn seal, can grab at the roof opening, lifting it away from the glass panel after years of usage, some still being original factory fitted ones. I had no option but to replace my original factory fitted seal, as it manage to detach itself from the glass panel, scrunch up, and get jammed inside the sliding roof opening. So at least now, saw the opportunity to getting round to treating the light rusting problem along the back edge of the glass metal frame, in which most of these cars all seem to suffer from.
